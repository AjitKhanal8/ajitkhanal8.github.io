The Imported Challenge: Economic Impact of Fresh Fruit and Vegetable Imports on US Producers

with G. Munisamy and D. Poudel

Journal of Agricultural and Applied Economics, 56(4), 544-574, 2024

Abstract: Domestic fruit and vegetable producers contend that rising imports during seasonal harvesting windows have negatively impacted domestic prices and revenue. This study simulates producers' revenue with the removal of above-average imports as defined by the U.S. International Trade Commission. Results indicate significant additional revenues to domestic producers in the simulated scenario. Also, additional revenues to producers by state and season show substantial heterogeneity with robustness checks revealing similar patterns. Options such as risk management and technological improvements are needed to enhance the competitiveness of U.S. fresh produce industries instead of limiting imports in the absence of illegal dumping.

Evaluating North American Dairy Trade Pre- and Post-USMCA Using a Quantitative Trade Model

with Jason H. Grant and Nicolas Legrand

Abstract: The United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA), effective in 2020, revised dairy trade provisions between the United States and Canada. However, disputes have arisen over the allocation of Canada's tariff rate quotas (TRQs). This paper analyzes the impact of the USMCA on US dairy exports to Canada by quantifying changes in unobservable trade costs using an international border indicator and estimated trade elasticity within a structural gravity framework. Our empirical analysis finds no economically or statistically significant change in Canada's trade costs facing U.S. dairy exports—either at the aggregate or individual product level—following the implementation of the USMCA on July 1, 2020. Moreover, we estimate that U.S. exports face higher trade frictions in Canada than in other major export destinations (eg, 65.1 percent higher than Mexico). Counterfactual simulations suggest that reducing U.S.-Canada export frictions to levels comparable to U.S.-Mexico would increase U.S. exports to Canada and Canadian exports to the U.S., albeit with a decline in intra-national trade. Export diversification toward emerging economies, coupled with targeted trade negotiations, could mitigate U.S. reliance on Canada's dairy market.

Trust in Hypothetical Blockchain Claims

with William Secor and Benjamin Campbell

Abstract: Trust in credence attribute claims and food-related information is essential to generate value from the work taken to create the claim. Blockchain technology may soon be another way in which claims can be managed along the supply chain. This study investigates whether the verification mechanism behind a claim on a product, including blockchain, and the owner of that mechanism affects consumers' trust in the claim. Using information treatments in a national survey, the results suggest that the method verifying a claim is not important in most cases unless the method is described to the consumer.

Distributional Assessment of the Impact of Imports and Exports on Net Farm Income

with Le Wang

In this project, we recognize that exposure to both imports and exports affects farm income. We construct a measure of exposure at the county level by taking into account a range of agricultural exports and imports across different countries. We employ an instrumental quantile regression model to quantify the effect across the entire income distribution.

Impact of Agricultural Imports on Farmland Rental Rates: A Causal ML Approach

with Le Wang

The motivation behind this project is the increasing farm size in the U.S. and declining farm numbers. We ask the following questions: 1) Does availability of new export markets cause lands of low-productive farms to be consolidated by more productive farms? 2) What is the effect of trade on land values across tenants and landowners?

Impact of Edge of Field Practices on Crop Productivity: An Event Study Approach

with Nicolas Legrand and Jason H. Grant

In 2015, the state of Minnesota introduced a buffer law under which farm fields bordering waters must maintain perennial vegetative buffers of up to 50 feet along lakes, rivers, and streams and buffers of up to 16.5 feet along ditches. Using county-level yield data and counties from neighboring states—Iowa, Wisconsin, South, and North Dakota—as controls, we assess the dynamic impact of the law on the yield of soybeans and corn.
